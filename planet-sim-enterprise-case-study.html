§ 04 / The Diagnosis

What was actually broken.

01

The post-demo gap.

AEs got strong reactions in demos. Then deals went quiet for weeks while the buying committee deliberated. The product was novel enough that prospects didn't have a vocabulary for it yet. The committee had nothing to feel, touch, or evaluate, so they defaulted to delay.

"We love what we saw, but we need to bring this to our committee."
"I can't really describe to them what I saw. Can you send something?"
02

Pricing negotiations.

Same problem we'd diagnosed at the SMB level: per-sensor, per-installation hardware-style pricing was triggering line-item negotiations instead of tier-selection conversations. At enterprise ACV, the negotiation surface was bigger and the cycle drag was longer.

+ Monthly 1:1s with each rep

§ 05 / Fix · Layer 01

Scale the SMB playbook into enterprise.

01 Port + scale
▼ Same insight, bigger trial, multi-stakeholder pricing

The motion ports. The mechanics scale.

The core SMB insight (case study 01) was that buyers in this category needed experiential proof in their own environment before they could move forward. The same insight applied to enterprise — just with bigger and more complex buying groups.

▼ FROM · SMB MOTION
Single-tower trial
  • 1 tower install
  • 3 named users (FM + 2 committee)
  • Real water data, day one
  • Per-flat SaaS tiering
  • Annual billing discount
→ scaled into
▼ TO · ENTERPRISE MOTION
Pilot install + multi-stakeholder access
  • Builder: pilot in 1 tower of flagship project
  • Committee: single-tower install, 3-5 named users
  • Project manager + facility lead + ops/tech rep
  • Per-flat tiering with volume discount at 200+ flats
  • Annual contracts
Why it attacked both bottlenecks
Post-demo silence shrank because the trial gave the champion something concrete to bring to committee — a working install in their own building. Pricing negotiation shrank because tier-selection is faster than component-by-component haggling.
§ 06 / Fix · Layer 02

AI-first sales enablement as the scaling lever.

02 System wiring
▼ Distinctive to the enterprise motion

Right asset. Right rep. Right language. Right cadence step.

With 4 SDRs and 12 AEs running cadences across 2 products and 2 buyer segments, the assets each rep needed at any moment varied a lot. Manually getting the right asset to the right rep was failing. Assets sat in shared drives nobody used during a live cadence.

▼ INPUT
Diagnosis
Gong NotebookLM HubSpot Zoho Win/Loss

Weekly Gong reviews caught new objections. NotebookLM synthesized ~20 win/loss transcripts into quarterly briefs informing pricing + messaging.

▼ ROUTING
n8n workflow
n8n Outreach

Routed enablement assets into Outreach cadences based on deal segment + cadence stage.

▼ OUTPUT
Asset library
Storylane Synthesia Battlecards ROI calc

Storylane demos by product × buyer type. Synthesia walkthroughs in regional languages. Battlecards + ROI tagged by segment + stage.

▶ ROUTING LOGIC
IF segment = builder AND product = Planet SIM AND cadence_step = 03
THEN send builder-specific Planet SIM Storylane walkthrough
Honest scope: the wiring itself was not exotic. The value was in getting the asset taxonomy right (segment × stage × product matrix took several iterations) and the timing logic (which assets fired at which cadence step). The infrastructure made it impossible for an SDR to send a generic asset when a segment-specific one existed.

§ 08 / The Byproduct

The number hardest to chart — and the one that mattered most.

Sales team confidence.

Selling a novel category into a market that doesn't believe it has a problem is demoralising work. Reps grind, deals stall, motivation drains.

Once the experiential trial was in place, the per-flat pricing was scripted, and the right asset showed up in the right cadence step automatically — the team's posture shifted. Deals flowed. Reps started recommending Planet SIM and Planet App into builder networks unprompted. SDR-to-AE handoffs got cleaner because both sides trusted the motion.

In a niche category, sales team confidence is a leading indicator of whether the motion will scale.

§ 10 / Hindsight

What I'd do differently.

01

Build the asset taxonomy before the workflow.

I designed n8n routing while the asset library was still being built. The taxonomy iterated three times before it was right, and each iteration meant rewiring n8n logic. Should have spent a clean two weeks on taxonomy first.

02

Run a more rigorous A/B on cadence variants.

We changed the cadence and the assets simultaneously. I can't cleanly attribute the ~40% cycle reduction to one or the other. Most was the trial + pricing reframe, but I'd love to know how much the AI-routed assets contributed independently.

03

Get NotebookLM into the workflow earlier.

I started using it as a synthesis layer about six months in. The first quarterly brief it produced changed how we positioned against legacy competitors. If I'd had that brief in month two, the messaging would have been sharper from the start.

§ 11 / The Takeaway

For product marketers reading this.

// Two PMM principles
PRINCIPLE 01

The best PMM ideas are usually replays.

When something works in one segment, the next move is figuring out which parts port and which need rebuilding. The SMB Touch-Feel-Convert motion didn't work in enterprise out of the box. It needed bigger trials, multi-stakeholder asset design, AI-routed cadence support. But the underlying insight — buyers in novel categories need experiential proof in their own environment — was the same. PMM is often less about invention and more about transposition.

PRINCIPLE 02

AI-first PMM in 2026 is about wiring, not generation.

The interesting use of AI here isn't that we drafted battlecards faster (we did). It's that we used n8n, Outreach, Storylane, Synthesia, Gong, and NotebookLM as a system that put the right asset, in the right language, in front of the right rep, at the right cadence moment. PMM in the next decade is about building these systems — not just producing the assets that go into them.
