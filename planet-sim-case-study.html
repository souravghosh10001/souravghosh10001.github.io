§ 01 / Summary

A brilliant solution trapped in hardware.

Planet SIM was built to help housing societies automate water management, increase efficiency, and cut costs. But our pricing was logically built on unit economics (per water sensor). This forced buyers to haggle over hardware instead of focusing on software value. By stepping out of the building and shadowing sales, I discovered the buyer’s true mental model: cost per flat, per month. We rebuilt our pricing around this insight, turning a hardware objection into a frictionless SaaS upgrade.

§ 02 / The Problem

The "Per-Sensor" Trap.

The architecture of the solution consisted of two parts: the software platform, and the physical IoT sensors installed in the building's underground and overhead water tanks.

Logically, we priced the solution based on our unit economics: customers paid for the exact number of sensors they needed. If a society had four towers, they paid for eight sensors. But this pricing model accidentally created a massive barrier.

Instead of talking about water savings or operational efficiency, buyers immediately fixated on the hardware. The buying committee would start haggling over sensor counts to cut costs: "We don't have water issues in the last tower, so can we just buy six sensors instead of eight?"

Because our solution required complete deployment to work optimally, this corner-cutting undermined the product. Worse, it completely demoralized the sales team. The entire sales conversation was hijacked by hardware haggling. The Value Proposition was lost in translation
§ 03 / The Investigation

Finding the buyer's mental model.

I knew we didn't have a product problem; we had a packaging and positioning problem. I got out of the building, shadowed the sales team, and started interviewing the buying committees. I asked a committee member how they evaluated other essential vendors, like lift maintenance. Their answers changed our entire trajectory:

"It’s just 5% of our monthly maintenance cost per flat, so it’s a no-brainer. Even if they increase it slightly, people don't mind paying a little extra per flat if it ensures their safety and convenience." The "Aha!" Moment

Housing society committees don't think in terms of hardware capital expenditure. Their entire mental model for budgeting is built around "cost per flat, per month." We were forcing our buyers to translate our per-sensor pricing into their per-flat budget.

§ 04 / The Pivot

Repackaging for the buyer's budget.

01 Pricing rebuild
▼ Aligning unit economics with buyer psychology

Sensors are our problem. "Per flat per month" is theirs.

I took this insight back to the team, and we completely restructured our GTM pricing model. We shifted from charging a lump sum per sensor to a SaaS-style subscription based entirely on a nominal fee per flat, depending on the tier.

₹29per flat / month

By making this shift, the pricing amounted to a very small fraction of a resident's existing monthly maintenance bill. It fit perfectly into how they already approved operational expenses.

§ 05 / The Impact

Hardware became a backend detail.

The shift in the sales motion was instantaneous. The buyers didn't care how the sausage was made, as long as they got the value they needed at a price that seamlessly fit their operational budget.

§ 06 / Strategic Overview

The PMM Takeaway.

// Bridging the Gap

When a sales team is struggling, the immediate instinct is often to build more features or train the reps harder. This case study proves that sometimes the product is fine, and the reps are fine, but the packaging is fundamentally at odds with the buyer's reality.

Your unit economics dictate your margins, but they should never dictate your customer-facing pricing model.

Strategic product marketing means getting into the field, uncovering the buyer's internal mental models, and translating your complex solution into the exact format they are already programmed to buy.
