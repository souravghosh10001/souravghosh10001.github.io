§ 01 / Summary

The product worked. The motion didn't.

We launched an IoT water platform into a market that didn't believe it had a problem. Six months of textbook B2B SaaS GTM produced a 10% lead-to-customer rate. Two pivots fixed it. Nine months later, conversion was 50%, CAC had dropped 80%, paid churn stayed flat at 0%, and 30% of customers upgraded organically. We took the same blueprint into 4 more metro cities.
Pivot 01  ·  15-day on-site trial replaces the demo
Pivot 02  ·  Per-flat SaaS pricing replaces per-sensor

§ 03 / The Problem

Two buyers. One product. Both stuck.

The buyer set was split. Same product, two very different sales motions, both stuck.

A
Enterprise Property Mgmt
Bought centrally, rolled across portfolios of residential complexes.
B
Housing Society Committees
Facility managers buying for a single complex.

The product itself: sensors on water tanks, real-time levels and pump status on a mobile app, predictive control to prevent shortages and overflows. The status quo? A security guard climbing tanks with a measuring stick.

Indian residential water management in 2022 was, quite literally, operating like it was 1995. — Field observation
§ 04 / The Diagnosis

It wasn't a product problem. It was a belief problem.

We started with the textbook B2B SaaS funnel: paid ads → MQL → SQL → online demo → sales call → close. The drop-off pattern was specific and informative.

§ 05 / Pivot 01

Touch. Feel. Convert.

01 Experiential GTM
▼ Replace the online demo with a 15-day on-site trial

Stop showing the product. Start letting it show itself.

Within 15 days of every successful trial, the same chain reaction kicked off. The facility manager would discover something they hadn't known. They'd surface it in a committee meeting. The committee would ask why the rest of the property wasn't on the system. The buying conversation flipped from us pitching to them asking.

MECHANIC 01
Single-tower install
Not the whole society. Limited risk for them, limited install cost for us.
MECHANIC 02
3 named users
Full app access for the facility manager + two committee members.
MECHANIC 03
Real water data
Their tanks. Their consumption. Their anomalies. No sandbox demo.
What buyers discovered in 15 days
"A specific tower had a hidden leak." · "Pumps were running 40% longer than needed." · "Peak consumption was 3 hours later than we'd assumed."
§ 06 / Pivot 02

Price how the buyer actually budgets.

02 Pricing rebuild
▼ Replace per-sensor hardware pricing with per-flat SaaS

Sensors are our problem. "Per flat per month" is theirs.

The original pricing was hardware-style: ₹9,000 per sensor up front + maintenance. Logical from a unit economics standpoint. A disaster from a sales standpoint. Every conversation became a negotiation over sensor count. We were positioned as a hardware vendor, not a SaaS platform.

Committee members didn't think in "sensors" or "towers." They thought in "per flat per month" — the unit of accounting in an Indian residential society, used for maintenance, security, cleaning.

▼ OLD · Hardware-style
₹9,000 per sensor + maintenance fee
→
Essential
₹49per flat / month

₹69per flat / month

₹99per flat / month
1–3% of existing per-flat maintenance

In societies where monthly maintenance was already ₹3,000–₹7,000 per flat, our pricing read as an addition to an existing line item — not a new category of spend. Sales conversations stopped being negotiations. They became tier selections.

Worked example
Essential plan × 120-flat society = ₹70,560 ARR. Materially higher than the original sensor-based deal — and predictable year over year.
§ 07 / Scope

What I owned vs. what the team owned.

PMM is a connective tissue role. Being precise here matters. I didn't write code, install sensors, or sign deals. I designed the GTM motion the team then ran.

◆ Mine (PMM)

  • Positioning & messaging architecture
  • Trial-as-GTM-motion proposal
  • Per-flat pricing model & tier design
  • Sales enablement: battlecards, objection guides, ROI calculator
  • Win/loss interview program
  • Cross-functional alignment with Product (trial UX) & Sales (cadence)

◇ Theirs

  • Product: Built trial provisioning flow + multi-tenant isolation for single-tower trials
  • Sales: Executed the new motion
  • Customer Success: Owned trial-to-paid handoff
  • Finance: Signed off on unit economics for new pricing
§ 08 / Hindsight

What I'd do differently.

01

Run a real win/loss program from week one.

We started one in month four. The signal from the first ten interviews was so strong it would have shortcut at least two months of guessing. I now treat structured win/loss as a day-one deliverable for any PMM engagement.

02

Instrument the trial harder.

We measured trial-to-paid but lacked clean cohort data on which in-trial behaviours predicted conversion. If I'd known on day three that a committee member hadn't logged in, I could have triggered a CS play. We caught some of this manually. It should have been a system.

§ 09 / The Takeaway

For product marketers reading this.

// PMM principle

The mistake we made for six months was assuming the funnel was broken because the funnel was broken. The funnel was broken because we were running the wrong motion for the buyer's actual psychology.

When the buyer doesn't believe the problem exists at the level the product solves, online demos and feature decks make the problem worse, not better. The product has to demonstrate itself in the buyer's environment, on the buyer's data, before the buying conversation can become productive.

And the pricing has to map to the buyer's mental model of how they spend money — not to your cost structure.

Both of those are PMM problems. Not Sales problems. Not Product problems. That's the reason this story is here.
